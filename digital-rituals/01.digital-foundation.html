Module 1

The Digital Foundation

Theme: “What you separate, you can protect.”

1.

Why this matters

Most online stress doesn’t come from hackers — it comes from

messy digital habits.

Using the same email for banking, shopping, gaming, and random app signups creates a single point of failure.
If that one account is hacked, or even just spammed to death, your whole digital life is exposed.
Upper-class households globally are heavy users of online services — streaming, shopping, food delivery, banking, bill payments, school portals. Without boundaries, everything gets entangled.
This module shows how to

separate critical from casual

so you gain resilience.
2.

The Core Principle

Imagine your digital life as a house. Would you give every guest the key to your master bedroom? Of course not. But that’s what happens when you use the same email and number everywhere.
Rule:

Create compartments.

Banking/government accounts in one box, shopping/entertainment in another, experiments and freebies in a third.
3.

Key Concepts Introduced

Primary email (Critical):

Only for banking, income tax, Aadhaar, PAN, EPFO, insurance, medical portals, school/college portals.

Secondary email (Everyday):

For Amazon, Netflix, Swiggy, Zomato, Flipkart, etc.
* **Tertiary “burner” email (Experiment):** For signing up to free courses, random apps, or contests where spam is expected. * **Phone number strategy:** Keep 1 mobile number linked to financial accounts. Use an alternate number (dual SIM, family spare, or even virtual number) for shopping and delivery apps.
### 4. What goes wrong when you don’t do this * A Flipkart leak compromises your banking email → phishing mail that looks like SBI, and you panic. * Spam and promo mails bury your bank alerts → you miss a fraudulent transaction until it’s too late. * SIM swap fraud succeeds because every OTP goes to your single overexposed number.
### 5. Step-by-step Implementation 1.

Create new Gmail IDs

* Gmail 1: [firstname.critical@gmail.com](mailto: firstname.critical@gmail.com) (never share casually).
* Gmail 2: [firstname.everyday@gmail.com](mailto: firstname.everyday@gmail.com) (for shopping, OTT).
* Gmail 3: [firstname.temp@gmail.com](mailto: firstname.temp@gmail.com) (for experiments and throwaway logins).
2. **Set strong passwords & 2FA** * Use your password manager right from the start. * Turn on Google Authenticator or phone prompt for each Gmail.
3. **Migrate accounts gradually** * Move banks, EPFO, income tax to Gmail 1. * Move Amazon, Zomato, Flipkart, Netflix to Gmail 2. * Sign up for random “free eBooks” or contests with Gmail 3.
4. **Assign phone numbers** * Keep your main SIM tied only to Gmail 1 and banking. * Use second SIM (or family spare) for shopping apps.
### 6. Practical Exercise * **Today’s Task:** Create two fresh Gmail accounts. * **Move at least 2 services** (one bank, one OTT/shopping app) to the correct email ID. * **Result:** Within a week, you’ll notice your “critical” inbox stays 90% cleaner.
### 7. Habit Anchor Every time you sign up for something new online, ask yourself: “Is this critical, everyday, or experimental?” → Use the right email/number.
### 8. Module 1 Quiz (for course feel) * Q1: Why is using one email ID for all accounts risky? * Q2: Which email ID should be used for your income tax login? * Q3: What’s the benefit of a “burner” email?
9.

Story Example

Rohit, a young banker, used the same Gmail for bank alerts and shopping deals. One day, his Gmail was phished after he clicked a fake Flipkart coupon link.
The hacker set up auto-forwarding of his mails, silently reading OTPs for weeks before siphoning ₹80,000. If Rohit had separated his accounts, the Flipkart leak would have stayed harmless.
