Module 2

Passwords Without Pain

“One key should not open every door.”

1.

Why this matters

Passwords are the locks of your digital house. But most people reuse the same 2 - 3 passwords across dozens of accounts. Once a leak happens — and leaks happen every day — attackers try that same password on banks, emails, and social media.
This is why simple habits around password management save you from the nightmare of identity theft, locked accounts, or drained bank balances.
2.

The Core Principle

Never reuse passwords.

Every account should have a unique, strong password. The catch is, no human can remember 100 different 16-character strings.
The solution: a password manager.
Think of it as your personal “digital locker” that remembers everything for you. You only remember one master password.
3.

Key Concepts Introduced

Password strength: Long, random, mixed characters. (“CorrectHorseBatteryStaple” type passphrases are stronger than “R0h!t123”).
Reuse risk: If your Flipkart password leaks, hackers test it on Gmail, SBI, Facebook, Paytm, and 50 other places.
Password manager: Apps like Bitwarden, 1Password, or NordPass generate and store secure passwords across devices. Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Even if your password is stolen, hackers need a second code (from SMS, authenticator app, or hardware key).
4.

What goes wrong when you don't do this

* Your old Zomato account leak reveals your common password. Attackers log in to your Gmail and reset everything else.
* A school portal hack exposes your child's login — you used the same password as your bank.
* An ex-employee at a service you once signed up for has a copy of the old password, which you're still using for Facebook.
5.

Step-by-step Implementation

Choose your password manager
* Free + secure option:
* Paid polished option:
* Install on phone + browser extension on your laptop.
Create your master password * Use a sentence or passphrase only you can remember.
* Example formula: 2 random words + year of a personal event + symbol.
* E.g., “OrangeTrain!2007Jump”
Add your first 5 accounts * Gmail (critical), Amazon (shopping), Netflix (OTT), Swiggy (food), Instagram (social).
* Let the manager generate new 16-character passwords for each.
Enable 2FA * Use Google Authenticator, Authy, or built-in phone prompts.
* Prioritize: Gmail, banking apps, social media, cloud storage.
Practice login flow * Log out, try logging in again — see how the password manager autofills safely.
* Train yourself to stop typing passwords manually.
6.

Practical Exercise

Today's Task: Install a password manager and secure 3 accounts with new strong passwords. Stretch Goal: Turn on 2FA for Gmail right away.
7.

Habit Anchor

Every time you sign up for a new service, let the password manager generate the password. Never invent one yourself.
8.

Module 2 Quiz

* Q1: Why is password reuse dangerous?
* Q2: What is the role of a password manager?
* Q3: If you only turn on 2FA for three services, which ones should they be?
9.

Story Example

A doctor in Delhi used the same password for Zomato, Gmail, and ICICI Bank. When Zomato was breached in 2017, hackers sold his login on the dark web.
Within weeks, someone logged in to his Gmail, intercepted OTPs, and emptied ₹1.2 lakh. Had he been using a password manager with unique logins, the Zomato breach would have remained harmless.
10.

Outcome of Module 2

By the end of this lesson, learners will have:
* Installed a password manager
* Created one strong master password
* Changed at least 3 major accounts to unique strong passwords
* Enabled 2FA on Gmail
