First 90 Days as an AI-Forward Leader

This module converts strategic understanding into immediate action. Leaders leave with a minimal, realistic, high-impact plan that does not depend on building models or hiring engineers.

Part 1: Three Immediate Moves (Days 1–30) These moves generate visible results quickly and build the muscle for deeper transformation.

Move 1: Pick One High-Volume Workflow and Automate It End-to-End Choose a workflow that is repetitive, rule-based, and carries significant time cost.

Examples:
Sales: daily lead qualification summaries, pipeline triage, follow-up drafting.
Support: first-draft customer replies, ticket routing, sentiment tagging.
HR: job description creation, screening questions, candidate summarization.

Finance: invoice categorization, expense checks, monthly reporting outlines.
Operations: shipment exception explanations, vendor follow-ups, daily ops summaries.

Start with a simple principle: automate the draft, keep humans as approvers.

Exercise (10 minutes): Leaders list five workflows their teams repeat daily. Pick the one with the highest “total workload × predictability × clarity.”

Write a one-sentence automation goal, such as:
"AI drafts follow-up emails for all inbound leads; sales reviews before sending."
This becomes the pilot.

Move 2: Run a Rapid Data Audit No technical audit needed. Only a leadership-level inventory that reveals risks and opportunities.

Ask four questions: What data do we use daily? Who owns it? Is it clean and consistent? Are we legally allowed to feed it to external AI tools?

Examples of immediate insights:
CRM fields have inconsistent formatting.
Sales notes are free-text chaos.
Invoice folders mix scanned PDFs with images.
HR docs store sensitive personal data without tagging.

Exercise (5 minutes): Pick one system (CRM, HRMS, ticketing, finance).
List the 3 biggest data problems your team faces.
Pick one problem the AI pilot should avoid depending on.
The insight: don’t automate workflows that run on dirty data until it’s cleaned.

Move 3: Form an AI Operating Committee That Meets Weekly This is not a bureaucratic committee. It’s a lightweight cross-functional group that assesses impact and removes blockages.

Composition: One person each from Sales, Ops, Finance, HR, and IT/Data. Meet for 20 minutes, once a week.

Agenda: What did the pilot achieve this week?
Where is friction?
What data or access is needed?
What is the next workflow to tackle?

Examples of decisions they might make:
Adding a QA step for AI-written customer emails.
Standardizing CRM notes to improve AI routing.
Allowing AI agents to schedule internal meetings.
Defining what approvals require human oversight.

No setup needed: a Calendar invite + shared doc is enough.

Exercise (5 minutes): Leaders draft the first meeting agenda and choose 2–3 people to invite. Write a one-line mandate for the group:

“Enable safe, meaningful automation across operations within 12 weeks.”

Part 2: Three Long-Term Moves (Days 30–90) These moves shape the organization’s AI-forward operating model.

Move 4: Align with a Primary Ecosystem (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Microsoft) You don’t need to commit to a model vendor in the first month, but you should commit to an ecosystem.

Examples of ecosystem decisions:

  • Use Microsoft Copilot across Office, Teams, SharePoint if you are on M365.
  • Use OpenAI if you want the fastest innovation and custom workflows.
  • Use Google if you already rely on Workspace, BigQuery, or Vertex.
  • Use Anthropic if you want stronger safety features and controlled outputs.

The key idea: selecting an ecosystem determines integration velocity, plug-ins, agent capabilities, and vendor lock-in.

Exercise (5 minutes): Leaders answer two clarifying questions:
Where does our work already happen (Microsoft? Google? Slack?)
Which ecosystem is moving fastest for our needs?
This produces a provisional ecosystem alignment.

Move 5: Redesign Roles Around “AI-First, Human-Proofed” Workflows As AI drafts or executes first passes, human roles shift upward toward supervision and creative judgment.

Examples:

  • HR shifts from sourcing resumes to evaluating AI-ranked shortlists.
  • Finance teams shift from manual reconciliation to reviewing flagged exceptions.
  • Sales teams shift from writing cold emails to approving AI suggestions and running more calls.
  • Operations move from manual vendor follow-ups to evaluating AI-created action queues.

What matters is not replacing people; it is reorganizing how human judgment is applied.

Exercise: Choose one role in your department.
Write down the tasks they do. Mark tasks as:
“AI drafts,” “AI runs autonomously,” or “Human decides.”
Rewrite the role description for a future where AI handles all “draft” tasks.
This becomes the basis for a modern job outline.

Move 6: Implement Lightweight Governance Avoid heavy frameworks. Use a simple three-tier rule:

AI must never autonomously do: Financial approvals, legal commitments, regulatory filings, performance ratings.

AI can autonomously do: Summaries, drafts, triage, prioritization, basic decisions with clear rules.

AI can do with supervision: Outbound communication, insights for strategic decisions, sensitive customer interactions.

Examples of immediate governance actions:

Create a shared “Do, Don’t, Review” table for all teams. Teach employees a 2-minute quality check routine before sending AI-generated content. Add an audit trail for AI-generated decisions where required.

Exercise (5 minutes): Leaders draft their own “Do / Review / Don’t” table for the pilot workflow.

In Closing

Leaders finish with a 90-day roadmap they can execute without technical help. A simple pilot, a clear committee, a clean data boundary, an ecosystem preference, early role redesign, and a no-nonsense governance layer create visible results within weeks.
