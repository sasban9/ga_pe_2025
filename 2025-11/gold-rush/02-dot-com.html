Dot-com build-out was a distribution shock. The internet became publicly visible, but most businesses had no online presence. Early adopters filled that gap and got paid.

The Trigger

1995–2000. Browsers became common. Domain names were cheap. Every company suddenly “needed a website.” But very few people knew how to build one.

The Demand Wave

Businesses asked for: Websites. Online catalogs. Email forms. Company portals. Nothing complex. Basic HTML + a server.

Scarcity of talent made even simple work high-value.

Who Made Money

  1. Web agencies building static sites at high margins.
  2. Hosting providers offering space on shared servers.
  3. Domain speculators buying and reselling names.
  4. Tool creators (early CMS, shopping carts).

The winners were not always the most technical. They were the first to productize the service.

Why It Was a Gold Rush

Distribution friction went to zero. Before: you needed print, phone, sales team. After: a website reached the world.

Anyone who understood:

  • “every business will need this”
  • “I can deliver something functional now”

made money fast.

Failure Pattern

Most internet startups tried to invent future behaviors (e.g., pets.com). They died.

The durable players sold infrastructure:

  • web hosting (GoDaddy)
  • online payments (PayPal)
  • search + ad network (Google)
  • marketplaces (Amazon)

They supported the ecosystem instead of trying to guess culture.

Parallel to 2025 AI

Dot-com → “every company needs a website.” 2025 → “every company needs an AI agent to cut operational drag.”

Early adopters again win by productizing something simple and universal:

  • lead follow-up
  • customer support triage
  • internal knowledge assistant
  • workflow automation

The same playbook applies:

  • Ship early
  • Solve boring but essential needs
  • Sell to businesses with money, not consumers
