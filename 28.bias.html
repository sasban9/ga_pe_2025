Weekend 28 Understanding and Responding to Discrimination/Bias

Here's a plan for a more comprehensive "Weekend 28 Understanding and Responding to Discrimination/Bias" lesson, designed for a 16-year-old, with an estimated duration of 60-90 minutes, allowing for discussion and activities.

Weekend 28: Understanding and Responding to Discrimination & Bias

Goal: To help the 16-year-old understand various forms of discrimination and bias, recognize their impact, and develop practical strategies for being an ally and responding constructively to prejudice.

Key Learning Objectives:

  • Define and differentiate between bias, prejudice, and discrimination.
  • Identify various forms of discrimination prevalent in society (e.g., based on religion, caste, gender, appearance, region, language, sexual orientation, disability).
  • Understand the individual and societal impact of discrimination.
  • Learn practical strategies for being an ally.
  • Develop constructive responses to prejudiced remarks or discriminatory acts.
  • Cultivate empathy and a commitment to social justice.

    • Materials Needed:

  • Whiteboard or large paper, markers/pens
  • Index cards or small slips of paper
  • Pens/pencils
  • (Optional but Recommended) Access to a computer/internet for quick research if questions arise.
  • (Optional) Short, age-appropriate video clips or news articles illustrating instances of discrimination.
    • Lesson Plan (60-90 minutes):

    Phase 1: Introduction & Definitions (10 minutes)

    1. Warm-up Question (5 min):
  • "Before we dive in, what comes to mind when you hear the words 'discrimination' or 'bias'?"
  • Allow them to share their initial thoughts. This helps gauge their current understanding.
    • 2. Defining Key Terms (5 min): Bias: "A tendency, inclination, or prejudice for or against something or someone. It can be conscious (explicit) or unconscious (implicit)." Example: "If I always assume people from a certain region are lazy, even without evidence, that's a bias." Prejudice: "A preconceived opinion that is not based on reason or actual experience; irrational dislike, suspicion, or hatred of a particular group." Example: "Holding a negative opinion about all people of a certain religion without knowing any of them personally."
    Discrimination: "The unjust or prejudicial treatment of different categories of people or things, especially on the grounds of race, age, sex, etc. This is the action based on prejudice."
    Example: "Refusing someone a job because of their caste, even if they are qualified." Analogy: "Think of it like this: Bias is a thought, prejudice is a strong negative feeling based on that thought, and discrimination is the action taken because of that feeling."

    Phase 2: Exploring Forms & Impact of Discrimination (20-25 minutes)

    1. Brainstorming Forms of Discrimination (10 min):
  • "Discrimination can manifest in many ways and target different groups. Let's list some categories or grounds on which people might be discriminated against."
  • Start with the ones you mentioned: religion, caste, gender, appearance.
    • Encourage them to think broadly:
    Religion: Discrimination based on faith or lack thereof.
    Caste: (Highly relevant in India) Discrimination based on inherited social hierarchy.
    Gender: Discrimination based on being male, female, or transgender. Appearance: Based on body size, skin color, facial features, clothing, etc.
    Region/Language: Discrimination against people from certain states or who speak a different language (e.g., North vs. South India, Assamese vs. Bengali speakers in certain contexts).
    Sexual Orientation: Discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals.
    Disability: Discrimination against people with physical or mental disabilities.
    Socio-economic Status: Discrimination based on wealth or poverty.
    Age: Ageism (e.g., assuming a younger person is inexperienced or an older person is slow). Write these down clearly.
    2. Impact Discussion (10-15 min):
  • "Now that we've identified these forms, let's discuss the impact. How does discrimination affect the individual who experiences it?"
  • Prompt questions: Emotional toll (sadness, anger, shame), psychological impact (anxiety, depression), reduced opportunities (education, jobs), safety concerns, loss of dignity.
  • "How does discrimination affect society as a whole?"
  • Prompt questions: Division, conflict, reduced productivity/innovation (loss of talent), perpetuation of stereotypes, erosion of trust, injustice, economic disparity.
  • Emphasize that discrimination doesn't just hurt the target; it harms everyone by limiting potential and fostering negativity.

    • Phase 3: Being an Ally & Constructive Responses (25-30 minutes)

    1. What is an Ally? (5 min):
  • "Being an ally means actively supporting and standing up for the rights of marginalized or discriminated groups, even if you are not part of that group yourself."
  • "It's about empathy â€“ putting yourself in someone else's shoes and acting on that understanding."
    • 2. Brainstorming "How to Be an Ally" (10-15 min):
  • "What are practical things we can do to be allies in our daily lives?"
  • Guide them to think beyond just direct confrontation:
    • Educate yourself: Learn about different cultures, histories, and challenges faced by various groups. Read books, watch documentaries.
    Listen to diverse voices: Seek out and amplify the voices of those who are marginalized.
    Challenge your own biases: Reflect on your own stereotypes and prejudices. Speak up (when safe): Don't let prejudiced jokes or remarks go unchallenged. Support inclusive practices: Advocate for fairness in school, sports, clubs. Be a role model: Demonstrate respect and kindness to everyone.
    Support affected individuals: Offer comfort and solidarity to those who have experienced discrimination. Report serious incidents: Know who to tell (teachers, school counselors, authorities) if you witness or experience severe discrimination.
    3. Constructive Responses to Prejudice (Role-Playing Activity) (10-15 min):
  • "It can be hard to know what to say or do when you hear a prejudiced remark or see an act of discrimination. Let's practice some responses."
    • Introduce the "ACT" framework (or similar simple framework):
    A Ask a clarifying question: "What do you mean by that?" "Can you explain why you think that?" (Puts the burden on them to explain their prejudice). C Connect with emotion: "That statement makes me uncomfortable/sad." "I feel that's unfair." (Uses "I" statements, less confrontational).
    T Tell the truth/Educate: "Actually, that's a harmful stereotype." "From my understanding, people from that community are very hardworking." (Provide a factual counterpoint). V Vocalize boundaries: "I don't find that funny." "Please don't use language like that around me." (Sets a clear limit).
    Scenario Cards: Write 3-4 simple, age-appropriate scenarios on index cards (e.g., "A friend makes a casteist joke," "Someone assumes a girl can't do well in science," "A relative comments negatively on someone's appearance").
    Practice: Have them pick a card and role-play how they would respond using one or more of the ACT strategies. Provide constructive feedback.

    Phase 4: Conclusion & Commitment (5 minutes)

    1. Recap Key Learnings:
  • "Today, we talked about different types of discrimination, their impact, and how we can be allies."
  • Reiterate the importance of empathy, social justice, and standing up for what's right.
    • 2. Personal Commitment:
  • "What's one thing you will try to do this week or month to be more mindful of bias or to be a better ally?"
  • Encourage them to think about a specific, actionable step.
    • 3. Reinforce: "It takes courage to challenge prejudice, but every small act makes a difference. Your voice matters."

    Tips for Facilitation:

  • Be Patient: This is a sensitive topic. Allow for silence and reflection.
  • Encourage Openness: Create a safe space where they feel comfortable sharing honest thoughts, even if they're grappling with their own biases. Avoid judgment.
  • Share Personal Anecdotes (Appropriately): If you have a relevant personal experience with bias or discrimination (as a target or an ally), sharing it can make the lesson more relatable and impactful.
  • Focus on Action: While understanding is crucial, the emphasis should shift towards what they can do.
  • Follow Up: In the coming weeks, casually bring up related news stories or observations to reinforce the lessons learned. Acknowledge and praise instances where they demonstrate allyship.
