A design philosophy or principle you resonate with.

What it means to include this:

This isn't about you being a professional designer (unless you are, of course!).

It's about showcasing your appreciation for how things are made, how problems are solved creatively, and how intentionality shapes our interactions with the world. It reveals:

Thoughtfulness and critical observation
You don't just consume products or experiences; you think about WHY they are designed the way they are.

Problem-solving aptitude: Design, at its core, is about solving problems – whether it's making a complex app intuitive, a chair comfortable, or a city livable. Resonating with a design philosophy implies you appreciate this problem-solving aspect.




Aesthetic and functional appreciation: You value both the beauty and the utility of well-designed things.

Ability to connect diverse fields: Design principles often transcend specific domains. By picking one, you show you can identify common threads across different areas.

Potential for innovative thinking: People who appreciate design often bring a fresh perspective to their own work, thinking about elegance, efficiency, and user experience.

How to "unpack" it in your video introduction

1. Name the philosophy/principle: Be specific. Don't just say "good design."

Minimalism: Less is more; focusing on essential elements.

User-Centric Design (UCD): Designing with the user's needs, behaviors, and motivations at the forefront.

Biomimicry: Innovation inspired by nature's designs and processes.
Affordance: The design of an object should suggest how it is used (e.g., a door handle that looks like it should be pulled).

Fitts's Law (in UI/UX): The time required to move to a target area is a function of the distance to and size of the target. (A bit more technical, but shows depth if relevant to your field).

Inclusive Design: Designing for the widest possible range of human diversity.

Sustainable Design: Designing products, services, and systems to minimize environmental impact.

"Form follows function" (Louis Sullivan): The shape of a building or object should primarily be based upon its intended function or purpose.

"Good design is as little design as possible" (Dieter Rams): Part of his 10 principles of good design.

2. Briefly define it (in your own words):

Show you understand it, don't just quote a definition.

3. Explain WHY you resonate with it:

This is the crucial part.

Connect it to your interests: "I'm drawn to MINIMALISM because I believe elegance often lies in simplicity and the removal of clutter, which is something I strive for in my code architecture/data visualizations/research papers."

Provide a specific example: "I'm fascinated by Biomimicry. For instance, the way engineers are studying the structure of abalone shells to create stronger ceramics, or how Velcro was inspired by burrs. It shows how elegant and optimized solutions can come from observing natural processes."

Relate it to a problem: "The principle of UCD deeply resonates with me. I've seen too many software applications that are technically brilliant but frustrating to use because the user wasn't at the heart of the design process. I believe placing the user first leads to truly impactful solutions."

Discuss its broader implications: "I admire the concept of Inclusive Design because it forces us to think beyond the 'average' user and consider the diverse needs of everyone. This kind of thoughtful consideration for accessibility and equity is something I believe is vital in all fields, not just design."
