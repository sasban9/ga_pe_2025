Software as a Service defined 2010–2024. Companies paid monthly subscriptions for tools — Salesforce, Slack, Notion, Figma — that helped humans do work faster. The human was still the unit of production.

The inflection
Then
GPT-4
arrived.
In 2023–2024, large language models crossed a threshold: they could complete cognitive tasks — drafting, coding, research, analysis, customer support — at near-human quality, at machine speed, for near-zero marginal cost.
The question stopped being "which tool should my employee use?" and became "do I need the employee?"
L
New paradigm
Labor
as a
Service
LaaS is a model where buyers procure completed work — not software seats, not FTEs — on demand. An AI agent (or a thin layer of AI + human oversight) delivers a business outcome billed by the task, the outcome, or the hour of compute.
✦ Output-oriented, not effort-oriented
✦ Infinitely scalable — no hiring lag
✦ Economics of software, quality of labor

The uncomfortable truth
What
happens
to us?
LaaS doesn't eliminate all human work. It restructures it. Three new roles emerge at scale:
01 Prompters & orchestrators — humans who specify and direct AI agents
02 Quality reviewers — humans who validate AI output for high-stakes decisions
03 Relationship owners — humans who sell, manage, and build trust with clients
The gap between these roles and displaced workers is vast — and bridging it is the defining policy challenge of 2026.
Incumbent threat
SaaS vendors
face a
reckoning.
The problem: If a customer can get the outcome directly — the written contract, the analyzed dataset, the coded feature — why pay for the tool that helps a human produce it?
SaaS companies are facing "outcome substitution" — customers don't cancel the software, they simply stop needing it because the agent bypasses the UI entirely.
The response: Every major SaaS vendor — Salesforce, HubSpot, Zendesk, Notion, Adobe — is racing to bolt AI agents onto their platforms and reframe themselves as LaaS providers.
The winners will own the workflow. The losers become infrastructure.
$
Business model revolution
Pricing
turns
inside
out.
SaaS pricing is predictable and recurring — the vendor's dream. LaaS pricing is usage-based and outcome-tied — the customer's dream. The power shifts.
→ Customers pay only for what they get
→ Vendors must guarantee quality to earn revenue
→ Gross margin compresses as compute scales
→ Moats become data + workflow lock-in, not UX
05
The shadow side
Risks &
failure modes.
⚠️
Hallucination liability — AI delivers wrong output; who owns the error in a no-FTE model?
⚠️
Data leakage — Customer inputs flow through third-party model APIs, raising IP and compliance risk
⚠️
Quality cliff — LaaS works for P90 tasks; the long tail of edge cases still breaks agents
⚠️
Commoditization race — If models are APIs, every LaaS layer is one better-prompted competitor away from displacement
⚠️
Regulatory backlash — Labor displacement at scale triggers political response, sector-specific AI restrictions
🤝
The missing piece
Trust is
the new
moat.
In SaaS, trust was earned by product reliability. In LaaS, trust is earned by output reliability — and that's far harder to establish and audit.
The LaaS companies that win will be those who build the most credible quality assurance layer: human-in-the-loop review, explainability tooling, audit trails, and SLA guarantees on output accuracy.
"HITL" — Human in the Loop — is not a temporary crutch. It is a permanent feature for high-stakes LaaS deployments.

The work
gets done.
The question

SaaS gave humans better tools. LaaS makes humans optional on a growing share of tasks. The companies, workers, and governments that adapt fastest — by redefining value, acquiring new skills, and building new guardrails — will define the next twenty years of the global economy.
SaaS → LaaS is not a trend. It is a regime change.
