Definition
What Is a Synthetic Teammate?
A Synthetic Teammate is an AI agent or system that participates autonomously in knowledge work — not merely assisting, but executing tasks, making decisions within defined limits, and collaborating alongside human team members in shared workflows.

The Problem
The Leadership
Playbook
Is Broken.
Legacy management training was designed for humans. Performance reviews, motivation theory, delegation, trust — these models were built on human psychology. Synthetic teammates break every assumption. Leaders who apply old playbooks to AI colleagues will fail — not because they lack intelligence, but because they lack a new mental model.

Critical Issue
Who Is
Accountable
When AI Fails?
AI has no consequences. It cannot be fired, sued, or shamed. Accountability always flows upward to humans. Leaders must define — before deployment — who owns each AI decision, what the escalation path looks like, and how errors are surfaced, learned from, and prevented.
Managing Performance
How Do You
Review an
AI Teammate?
  • Output quality — accuracy, relevance, hallucination rate
  • Reliability — consistency across edge cases and contexts
  • Efficiency — tokens, latency, cost per task completed
  • Safety — adherence to guardrails and ethical constraints
  • Collaboration fit — how well it integrates into human workflows
Unlike humans, AI teammates do not self-motivate or self-improve without human redesign. Performance management means prompt engineering, fine-tuning, and system redesign.
11
Delegation Science
Delegate
the Task,
Not the Judgment.
The golden rule of hybrid delegation: AI executes, humans decide in ambiguity. Any task with a clear success metric and bounded inputs is fair delegation territory. Any task requiring moral reasoning, relationship nuance, or creative risk-taking demands human lead. Map your team's work against this axis before assigning.
💬
Communication Design
AI Doesn't Read
Between the Lines.
Human communication relies on context, relationship history, and unspoken norms. AI has none of these. Leaders must train their teams to communicate with AI precisely — and to recognize when vagueness will produce garbage output. Clarity is not optional in a hybrid team.
Prompt Literacy Explicit Instruction Feedback Loops
Organisational Culture
Culture Doesn't
Scale to Silicon.
Culture — the invisible rules that govern how teams behave — is built by humans, for humans. But AI behaviour shapes culture too: it models work norms, sets speed expectations, and can inadvertently reward volume over depth. Leaders must consciously defend culture against AI drift.
  • Establish AI-free zones for creativity and reflection
  • Celebrate human judgment, not just AI-enabled speed
  • Name and address AI's effect on psychological safety
14
Ethics & Integrity
Bias
Without a Face.
AI systems inherit the biases of their training data — and their outputs carry authority because they appear objective and tireless. Leaders must actively audit for bias, build diverse review processes, and resist the temptation to outsource ethical judgment to an algorithm.
"An AI that discriminates quietly is more dangerous than one that does so loudly."
