Here’s a full-fledged course design for “Capital vs Labor in the Age of AI”, written in a way that makes it engaging, reflective, and practical.
Course Title

Capital vs Labor in the Age of AI:

Power, Wealth, and Work in a Machine World

Course Overview

For centuries, wealth creation has relied on two great forces: capital, which compounds over time, and labor, which trades time for money. The industrial age tilted the scales toward capital.

The digital age accelerated it. Now, in the age of AI, the question resurfaces: What happens to labor when machines can think, act, and produce?
This course explores the deep dynamics of capital and labor, why inequality may widen further, and how societies, companies, and individuals can reshape the balance.

Learning Objectives

By the end of the course, students will:
* Understand the historical evolution of capital and labor power.
* Analyze how AI shifts productivity, ownership, and compensation.
* Critically assess new labor models—gig work, creator economies, AI-assisted entrepreneurship.
* Explore economic, ethical, and political debates around inequality.
* Imagine future scenarios for wealth distribution in an AI-driven world.
* Develop practical strategies to stay relevant, resilient, and empowered.
Course Structure (8 Modules)

Module 1: The Origins of Capital and Labor

* Labor as survival: hunter-gatherers and agrarian toil.
* The rise of capital in trade, industry, and empire.
* Compounding wealth vs non-compounding labor.
* Why the debate matters more in AI times.
Module 2:

The Industrial Age Legacy

* Factories and wage labor.
* The great promise of unions and worker rights.
* Capitalists: from merchants to barons of steel and oil.
* Lessons from the 19th - 20th century labor struggles.
Module 3:

The Digital Revolution

* Computers and globalization.
* The knowledge worker: labor moving “up the value chain.”
* Platforms (Google, Amazon, Facebook) and the rise of capital's dominance again.
* Inequality as an algorithmic feature, not a bug.
Module 4:

AI as the Great Disruptor

* AI replacing routine mental and physical labor.
* The exponential power of AI when paired with capital.
* How ownership of data and models determines power.
* “Labor without laborers”: when machines generate wealth.
Module 5:

New Forms of Labor

* Gig economy vs algorithmic bosses.
* Creator economy: labor as personality, creativity, uniqueness.
* AI as co-pilot: augmented labor rather than displaced labor.
* The question: can labor ever compound like capital?
Module 6:

The Future of Capital

* Who owns AI: corporations, governments, or communities?
* Decentralized capital—crypto, DAOs, shared ownership models.
* Capital concentration: trillion-dollar companies vs the individual worker.
* Possible redistributions: UBI, taxation, or profit-sharing models.
Module 7:

Ethics, Power, and Policy

* Moral dilemmas of wealth concentration.
* The state's role: regulation or acceleration?
* Worker rights in an AI-mediated economy.
* Political ideologies reborn: socialism, techno-feudalism, libertarianism.
Module 8:

Navigating the New World

* How individuals can survive and thrive.
* Building “compoundable labor”: reputation, networks, digital products, ownership stakes.
* Case studies of individuals and groups leveraging AI to escape wage traps.
* Final reflections: will labor and capital reconcile, or will AI widen the divide?

Pedagogical Style

The course mixes history, economics, and philosophy with practical strategies. Each module closes with:

* Case Study (historical or current example).

* Reflection Prompt (“If your job was automated tomorrow, how would you reinvent your labor?”).
* Exercise (e.g., simulate designing a labor model where workers get equity instead of wages).

Target Audience

* Students of economics, sociology, and business.
* Professionals facing automation anxiety.
* Entrepreneurs building the future of work.
* Policy-makers and social thinkers.
Suggested Duration
8 weeks (one module per week) or condensed into a 2-day intensive seminar.
